Church Architecture

The Cologne Cathedral, dedicated to St. Peter and the Virgin Mary, was designed in the tradition of the High Gothic cathedrals of northern France.

Its structure is richly detailed, though somewhat formal in execution, reflecting an almost scientific precision in both design and craftsmanship. The cathedral stretches 144.6 meters in length and rises to about 45 meters in height.

While its ground plan draw clear inspiration from Amiens Cathedral, it also incorporates distinctive features that set it apart from its French counterparts.

The Cologne Cathedral features an eight-bay nave and a four-bay choir, each designed with double aisles‚Äîthe outer aisles slightly narrower than the inner ones. Both arms of the transept contain four bays with aisles on the east and west sides. The two west towers, each built over four aisle bays, are larger and deeper than those in French cathedrals, resulting in their impressive height.

At the eastern end, the chevet includes an ambulatory with seven radiating chapels, and the entire structure is covered with quadripartite rib vaults. While the elevation and cross-section resemble Amiens Cathedral, Cologne‚Äôs design uses slimmer and more numerous vertical supports, creating a more intricate look. The arcade piers have molded surfaces and small foliage capitals, and the vault shafts extend seamlessly upward. The triforium and clerestory are joined by continuous mullions, each bay containing four windows. The overall height matches Amiens, but the triforium is positioned lower, dividing the elevation in a 3:1:3 ratio.

Interior Sculptures

Inside the Cologne Cathedral, stone figures of Christ, the Virgin Mary, and the Twelve Apostles adorn the choir piers. Each statue stands about 2 meters high beneath ornate baldachins, roughly 5 meters above the floor. Except for Christ and the Virgin, the baldachins are topped with angels playing musical instruments. Created in the cathedral workshop, these sculptures follow French Gothic styles, particularly those of Sainte-Chapelle in Paris, with richly patterned drapery later renewed in the 19th century.

The choir preserves many medieval furnishings, including the Gero Crucifix‚Äîthe earliest surviving life-size wooden crucifix from around the 10th century‚Äîand the oak choir stalls made between 1308 and 1311, which have 104 seats. The high altar sculptures, created by artisans from Lorraine, Cologne, and Paris, show regional stylistic influences. Despite external collaborations, the cathedral workshop developed its own local style that shaped Cologne‚Äôs sculpture into the 14th century.

Stained Glass

The stained glass windows of the Cologne Cathedral include original medieval works from the choir chapels, clerestory, and north nave aisle. Much of the colored glass was removed in the 18th century and replaced with white panes, but during the 19th-century Gothic Revival, medieval glass from other Cologne churches was reinstalled, especially in the north transept and sacristy.

Treasury

The Cologne Cathedral Treasury is the largest and richest in Germany, originally formed around its collection of relics, which were considered the true treasures in the Middle Ages. Located in the north transept, it houses chalices, monstrances, reliquaries, vestments, manuscripts, and other sacred objects‚Äîmany still used for worship.
